*Final Fantasy VII Remake is not the game I expected. It's the game that made me realize I didn't know what I actually wanted from a remake.*

There's a moment about twelve hours in where the camera pulls back during a walk through the Sector 5 slums, and the game just lets you exist in Midgar for a while. No combat, no cutscene trigger — just the hum of machinery overhead, kids running past, and Aerith talking about flowers like they matter more than anything. I'd been bracing for nostalgia. What I got instead was something closer to homesickness for a place I'd never actually lived in.

I came into this as someone who played the original at fourteen and remembered it in fragments — the Shinra building elevator, the motorcycle chase, the specific dread of the plate falling. I wasn't precious about it. I didn't want a 1:1 recreation. But I also wasn't prepared for how aggressively this game would insist on being its own thing while wearing the skin of something I already knew.

<div class="break">* * *</div>

The combat is what carried me through the first half and what kept pulling me back after I'd put it down. It's a hybrid system — real-time action layered over an ATB gauge that fills as you attack, dodge, and block — and on paper it sounds like the kind of design-by-committee solution that shouldn't work. In practice, it's one of the best combat systems Square Enix has ever built.

What makes it click is that the ATB gauge forces you to stay engaged. You can't just mash attack and win. You need to pressure enemies, stagger them, and then spend your accumulated gauge on abilities, spells, or items at exactly the right moment. Switching between party members isn't optional — it's the rhythm of the system. Cloud plays differently from Barret, who plays differently from Tifa, and the game expects you to flow between all of them mid-fight without pausing.

Hard mode, which locks you out of items and removes MP recovery between fights, turns this into something genuinely demanding. Every Cura has a cost. Every Phoenix Down you don't have is a decision you made three rooms ago. It's the closest this game gets to the resource management that made the original's materia system feel consequential.

<blockquote class="pull-quote">
  <p>"The combat didn't make me nostalgic for the original. It made me wonder why turn-based ever felt like the only way to do this."</p>
</blockquote>

The materia system itself is simplified but still satisfying. Slotting Elemental into your weapon versus your armor changes how you approach every encounter. Pairing magnify with healing spells turns Aerith into a walking triage unit. There's enough depth to build around, even if it never reaches the combinatorial madness of the original's linked slots.

Where the game stumbles mechanically is in its pacing between fights. The side quests are, almost without exception, filler. Find someone's cats. Kill three monsters in a junkyard. Deliver something. They're MMO-grade fetch quests dressed up with decent character writing, and they break the momentum of a story that otherwise moves with real urgency. I did all of them because I wanted the materia and the weapon upgrades. I resented most of them.

The thing I kept thinking about between sessions wasn't the combat, though. It was the way this game treats its own source material. Without getting into spoiler territory, Remake is aware that you've played the original. It knows what you expect. And it spends its final hours making an argument — not always a coherent one — that meeting those expectations isn't the point. There are sequences in the last chapter that feel less like a remake and more like a conversation between two versions of the same story, disagreeing about what matters.

I found this genuinely exciting the first time through. On reflection, I think the execution is messier than the ambition. Some of the late-game imagery leans hard on spectacle without earning the emotional weight it's reaching for. The final boss gauntlet is visually overwhelming but emotionally scattered — it's trying to land three different kinds of climax at once and doesn't fully commit to any of them.

<div class="break">* * *</div>

But here's what stays with me: the Midgar in this game feels like a real place in a way the original never achieved. The pizza — that massive plate overhead — isn't just a plot device anymore. You feel its weight. You see how people live under it, how they talk about it, how they've stopped noticing it. The environmental storytelling is some of the best Square Enix has done, and it makes the moments of destruction hit harder because you've walked those streets.

The character work is similarly elevated. Jessie, Biggs, and Wedge were barely characters in the original. Here they're fully realized people with motivations, humor, and enough screen time that you actually care when things go wrong. Barret's anger has texture to it now. Tifa's conflict between loyalty and doubt is no longer subtext — it's the engine of half her scenes. Even Cloud's mask-of-cool persona is handled with more nuance; the game lets you see the cracks without rushing to explain them.

Aerith, though. Aerith is the heart of this game in a way that goes beyond what the original asked of her. She carries a knowledge that the game never fully explains but constantly implies, and her scenes with Cloud have a quality I can only describe as someone trying to enjoy something they know is temporary. It's devastating once you realize what the game is doing with her, and it recontextualizes almost every interaction.

<div class="landing-note">
  <p><strong>One last thing.</strong></p>
  <p>
    This is a 40-hour game that covers what was originally a 6-hour section of a much larger story, and whether that bothers you will depend entirely on what you came here for. If you wanted a faithful, efficient retelling, this will frustrate you — it lingers, it detours, it adds hours of content that didn't exist before. If you're open to a game that uses a familiar framework to tell a story that's arguing with its own history, this is one of the most interesting things Square Enix has made in years. The side quests are weak. The final chapter overreaches. But the 35 hours between those complaints are some of the best the series has ever been, and I'd rather a remake that swings too hard than one that plays it safe.
  </p>
</div>
